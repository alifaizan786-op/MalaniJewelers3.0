Nature's Beautiful Magic

Real pearls are examples of nature's beautiful magic. They are formed when a mollusk (oyster, clam, mussel) has an irritant enter its shell; in defense, the mollusk produces layers of fluid (known as nacre, pronounced NAY-kur) around the irritant. The process takes between 5-10 years, and the result is the beautiful luminous gems we cherish as pearls.

Types of Pearls

Let's review the three types of pearls you may consider when shopping for pearl jewelry:

Natural Pearls

Natural pearls, those that form organically in nature, are extremely rare - very few are in the market today. The best source for natural pearls was the Persian Gulf, but when oil was discovered around the 1930s the Gulf waters could no longer produce pearls. Today, traditional fishing for natural pearls is still practiced in India - but the pearls harvested are often small and expensive.

Cultured Pearls

Cultured pearls make up the majority of pearls on the market today. The "culturing" process dates back to the late 19th century. Cultured pearls are formed in the same way as natural pearls, and are considered real pearls. In cultured pearls, an irritant is surgically placed into the mollusk and protected in "pearl farms" while the pearl develops. Of the pearls created after a five-to-ten year farming cycle, only 5% are of the high quality required for fine jewelry.

Imitation Pearls

Imitation pearls are the third type and have no connection to the natural pearl making process. They are made from glass beads that are dipped into a solution made from fish scales. While most have a high luster, it may eventually fade. Professional jewelers can easily spot imitation pearls and will not sell you imitation marked as real.

Pearl Quality Factors

Pearls are classified by origin, then graded by size, shape, nacre thickness, color, luster, surface clarity and how they match. Here are the key quality factors to consider:

The combination of surface brilliance and deep-seated glow. Good-quality cultured pearls should be bright, not dull. Your reflection should be clearly visible on the surface.

Shape

Perfectly round pearls are most valuable, but baroque (irregular) shapes can be equally beautiful and more affordable while still maintaining excellent quality.

Size

Measured by diameter in millimeters. Average cultured pearls are 7-7.5mm. Generally, larger pearls are more valuable, but quality factors matter more than size alone.

Refers to the absence of disfiguring spots, bumps or cracks. Clean surfaces have higher value, though minor imperfections are natural and acceptable.

Quality Pearl Strands

When evaluating pearl strands, look for these quality indicators:

  • Pearls should not bunch or twist when laid flat
  • Knots should be shaped uniformly and pushed snugly against both sides of every pearl
  • Silk cord should match the pearl color as closely as possible
  • Each pearl should be individually knotted to prevent loss if the string breaks
  • The clasp should be secure and complement the pearl quality

How to Clean & Store Pearls

Proper care ensures your pearls maintain their beauty and luster for generations:

Daily Care

  • Apply cosmetics, hair sprays and perfume before putting on pearl jewelry
  • Wipe pearls with a soft cloth after wearing to remove traces of cosmetics
  • Put pearls on last, take them off first
  • Avoid contact with chemicals and abrasives

Cleaning

  • Wash with mild soap and water when needed
  • Never use chemicals, abrasives or solvents
  • Use only soft, damp cloth for cleaning
  • Allow to air dry completely before storing

Storage

  • Always lay pearl strands flat to dry - never hang them
  • Store in chamois bag or wrap in tissue
  • Keep separate from other jewelry to prevent scratching
  • Avoid tossing carelessly into purses or jewelry boxes

Maintenance

  • Restring pearls annually to prevent thread weakening
  • Ensure pearls are knotted between each pearl
  • Have clasps checked regularly for security
  • Consult professional jewelers for repairs
